Perspectives from our new DDLS Co-Directors Carolina Wählby and Erik Kristiansson

As the SciLifeLab and Wallenberg National Program for Data-Driven Life Science (DDLS) enters its next phase, our two new Co-Directors, Carolina Wählby (Uppsala University) and Erik Kristiansson (Chalmers/University of Gothenburg), reflect on strategy, training, interdisciplinarity, technology, and inspiration.

Trio awarded the Areas of Advance prize for work on antibiotic resistance

The Areas of Advance prize 2025 is awarded to Erik Kristiansson, Michaela Wenzel and Fredrik Westerlund for their work to further develop and strengthen the Centre for Antibiotic Resistance Research (CARe).

Multi-resistance in bacteria predicted by AI model

An AI model trained on large amounts of genetic data can predict whether bacteria will become antibiotic-resistant. The new study shows that antibiotic resistance is more easily transmitted between genetically similar bacteria and mainly occurs in wastewater treatment plants and inside the human body.

Two million SEK from the Swedish Research Council to evaluate AI-based diagnostic methods in healthcare

The Swedish Research Council has decided which applications have been awarded grants for proof of concept in the field of infection in 2024. Erik Kristiansson and Anna Johnning from Mathematical Sciences in Gothenburg have been awarded grants for AI-based diagnostic methods in healthcare.

New AI diagnostics can help prevent the spread of resistant bacteria

Antibiotic resistance is a serious global threat to public health. This week, WHO's Antibiotic Resistance Week has been highlighted in several ways, including on chalmer.se. Anna Johnning and Erik Kristiansson and colleagues are researching new ways to detect and map resistant bacteria and prevent their spread.

Swedish Radio highlights diagnostic methods in AI

Anna Johnning, Erik Kristiansson and colleagues are researching new ways to detect and map resistant bacteria and prevent their spread. On Monday this week, Anna Johnning was interviewed by Swedish Radio P4. Listen to the feature from minute 24:00.

International interest in AI method that detects toxic chemicals

Erik Kristiansson and Mikael Gustavsson, at the Department of Mathematical Sciences, have together with colleagues developed an AI method that can identify toxic chemicals, based solely on knowledge of the molecular structure. The results have been reported by, among others, American MSN, Japanese Note and German Chemie.

Swedish Television highlights research on previously unknown antibiotic resistance

A new study from Chalmers University of Technology and the University of Gothenburg shows that genes that make bacteria resistant to antibiotics are much more widespread in our environment than was previously realised. On Saturday, Erik Kristiansson was interviewed on SVT Nyheter about the implications of these findings.

Erik Kristansson on Highly Cited Researchers list

​Three researchers from Chalmers can be found on Clarivate's "Highly Cited researchers list" in 2022 – a list of the most cited, and thereby most influential, researchers in the world. One of them is Erik Kristiansson, professor at the Department of Mathematical Sciences.

Economic interests cloud hazardous chemicals reductions

In a recently published study Mikael Gustavsson and Erik Kristiansson have worked together with Jessica Coria at the Scool of Business, Economics and Law at University of Gothenburg. They have analysed different data sets regarding EU:s regulation of hazardous chemicals and the results show that economic interest has played a large role in shaping the regulations.
